📊 Better Validation Reports

The validator now writes reports like a strategy consultant, not a search engine.

What changed

Reports tell a story instead of listing facts

The AI now writes executive summaries using a three-act narrative: first it mirrors the founder's world, then it builds tension around the core risk, then it presents the path forward. Each report weaves 2-3 "win themes" throughout, so when a founder shares it with an investor, it reads like a pitch — not a book report.

Before
"Your market size is $2.4B. The competitive landscape is moderate."
After
"Dental practices are drowning in paper. Here's why the $2.4B market is about to tip — and why your timing is perfect."
What changed

Scoring is evidence-based, not just AI guessing

Claims backed by real sources score full marks. Founder assumptions get discounted to 80%. Pure AI guesses get 60%. The report flags four types of bias (confirmation, survivorship, anchoring, optimism) so founders see where they might be fooling themselves.

Before
All claims treated equally — no distinction between hard data and wishful thinking
After
Each claim tagged as "cited source," "founder claim," or "AI-inferred" with automatic score weighting

Sarah is building a B2B SaaS for dental clinics. She runs the validator. The report recommends content marketing and community for her stage (pre-PMF) — not paid ads, which would waste her budget. It flags her $2.4B TAM claim as "AI-inferred" and discounts it, noting optimism bias. The executive summary tells a compelling three-act story she can forward directly to her advisor.

Result: Report she can actually share with investors

🚀 Smarter Growth Advice

The AI now knows which growth channels work at which stage — and stops recommending things that don't fit.

What changed

Channel recommendations match your startup stage

Pre-PMF founders get told to do customer interviews and content — not Facebook ads. Post-PMF founders get told to invest in paid acquisition and partnerships. Each channel recommendation comes with an ICE score (Impact, Confidence, Ease) so founders know what to try first.

What changed

Growth experiments are designed like a pro would design them

Instead of vague "try social media" advice, the AI designs complete experiments with a hypothesis, baseline metric, target metric, timeline, and success/fail threshold. Same format Y Combinator teaches.

Jake's fintech app has 500 signups but only 12% complete onboarding. He opens Growth Strategy mode. The AI identifies activation as the #1 bottleneck (not acquisition — he doesn't need more signups, he needs the ones he has to activate). It designs an experiment: "Reduce onboarding from 7 steps to 3. Target: 25% activation within 2 weeks. Success threshold: 20%+."

Result: One clear experiment instead of 10 vague ideas

🎤 Pitch Practice That Actually Helps

Founders can now rehearse investor meetings with an AI that asks the same hard questions real VCs ask.

What changed

The AI acts like a real investor, not a cheerleader

Practice Pitch mode runs a structured session: deliver your 60-second pitch, get scored on 5 dimensions (Clarity, Urgency, Differentiation, Ask, Confidence), face 3 hard investor questions tailored to your startup data, get coached on your weakest answer, then re-deliver. The AI gives one improvement at a time — never an overwhelming list.

Priya is pitching her AI supply chain tool next week. She opens Practice Pitch and delivers her elevator pitch. Score: 6/10 — strong on clarity, weak on urgency. The AI asks: "What happens to your customers' margins if they don't solve this in the next 12 months?" Priya stumbles. The AI coaches her on quantifying the cost of inaction. She re-delivers: "Supply chain delays cost mid-market manufacturers $2.3M per year in excess inventory. We cut that by 60% in 90 days."

Result: Score jumps from 6/10 to 8/10 in one session

⚔️ Investor Pipeline Gets Real

No more gut-feel investor tracking. Every conversation gets scored.

What changed

Each investor scored on 8 dimensions, out of 40

The MEDDPICC framework — used by the best enterprise sales teams in the world — now scores every investor conversation. It checks: Do they have the budget? Is there a champion? Is there a compelling event driving urgency? Are you talking to the decision maker? Verdicts are brutal and honest: Strong, Battling, At Risk, or Unqualified.

What changed

Outreach emails write themselves

When a founder reaches out to an investor, the AI writes the email using proven cold email anatomy: signal-based opening (reference something specific), one-sentence value prop, low-friction CTA. Timing is based on buying signals — a fund that just raised gets contacted this week, a partner who spoke at a conference gets contacted within 2 weeks.

Aisha has 8 investor conversations in her CRM. She opens Deal Review mode. The AI inspects each one: "Sequoia — you've only spoken to an associate. No access to the decision-making partner. Score: 14/40 — Unqualified. Either get a GP meeting in 7 days or deprioritize." Then: "Lightspeed — partner engaged, they have a portfolio gap in your space, but no paper process started. Score: 28/40 — Battling. Next step: ask about their term sheet timeline this week."

Result: Stops wasting time on dead-end conversations

🎯 Sprint Board That Thinks

Tasks are now scored, sequenced, and designed to build momentum.

What changed

Every task gets a RICE score

Tasks are scored by Reach (how many users affected), Impact (how much it moves the needle), Confidence (how sure we are), and Effort (how long it takes). High-impact, low-effort tasks float to the top. This is the exact same framework product managers at Airbnb and Shopify use.

What changed

Sprints start with quick wins, not the hardest task

The Kano model classifies tasks as Must-Have, Performance, or Delighter. Must-haves go in Sprint 1. Delighters go later. And each sprint starts with a quick win — because momentum matters more than perfection when you're building fast.

📝 Business Model Coach

The Lean Canvas coach now fixes the weakest part of your business model first.

Tom's lean canvas says Customer Segments: "Small businesses." The Canvas Coach flags this as the weakest box and asks: "Which small businesses? What industry? What size? What specific pain makes them search for a solution today?" Tom refines to: "Independent restaurants with 1-3 locations doing $500K-$2M revenue, struggling with food waste costing 8-12% of revenue." The coach responds: "Now we're talking. 4 of 9 boxes are strong — you're 44% there. Let's tackle Revenue Streams next."

Result: Vague canvas becomes specific and testable
