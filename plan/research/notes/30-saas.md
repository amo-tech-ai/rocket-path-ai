Common Mistakes in SaaS
Avoid these pitfalls that derail saas startups.

Building features before validating demand
70% of SaaS founders
Months of wasted development, product-market fit delays

Prevention: Use landing pages, waitlists, and pre-sales to validate before building. Talk to 50+ potential customers first.

Underpricing to compete on cost
55% of SaaS startups
Unsustainable unit economics, can't afford sales/marketing

Prevention: Price on value, not cost. Test 3x your 'comfortable' price. Enterprise customers expect to pay for quality.

Ignoring churn until it's critical
60% of SaaS companies
Growth stalls, LTV collapses, fundraising becomes impossible

Prevention: Track cohort retention from day 1. Build customer success before you think you need it. Fix churn before scaling acquisition.

Trying to serve everyone
65% of early-stage SaaS
Diluted positioning, slow sales cycles, feature bloat

Prevention: Pick a narrow ICP and dominate it. Say no to customers outside your focus. Expand segments only after proving one.

Frequently Asked Questions
How do I validate a SaaS idea before building?
IdeaProof validates SaaS with analysis of: recurring revenue potential, churn predictions, CAC, LTV, market size, competition, technical feasibility, and monetization models (freemium, subscription, usage-based).

What makes SaaS validation different?
SaaS validation requires analyzing: subscription economics (MRR/ARR), churn rate predictions, customer lifetime value, pricing tiers optimization, freemium vs paid conversion rates, and scalability.

Should I build freemium or paid-only SaaS?
Freemium works for: viral potential, network effects, low support costs. Paid-only works for: complex products, high value, enterprise focus. IdeaProof provides data-backed recommendations.

What's a good CAC for SaaS?
Rule: CAC should be < 1/3 of LTV. For B2B SaaS: $200-$1,000 CAC acceptable. For B2C SaaS: $10-$200 CAC target.

What SaaS metrics should I track?
Essential: MRR/ARR, CAC, LTV, Churn rate, ARPU, Time to value, Activation rate, Feature adoption, NPS, Logo retention.

What's the ideal MRR to raise funding?
Seed funding: $10K-$50K MRR. Series A: $100K-$250K MRR + strong growth (15-20% MoM). Growth rate, retention, and market size also matter.

What is the global SaaS market size?
Global SaaS market was $197 billion in 2023, projected to reach $380 billion by 2028 (14% CAGR). Over 30,000 SaaS companies worldwide.

What's a good SaaS churn rate?
Monthly churn targets: SMB 3-5%, Mid-market 1-2%, Enterprise <1%. Annual churn: 5-10% (good), <5% (excellent). Logo vs revenue churn both matter.

What conversion rate should I expect from freemium?
Freemium to paid: 2-5% is typical. Top performers: 7-10%. B2B often higher than B2C. Focus on activation metrics to improve conversion.

How long should my SaaS free trial be?
7-day: Simple products, urgency. 14-day: Most common, balanced. 30-day: Complex products. No trial: High-value enterprise. Test different lengths.

What's the average SaaS startup valuation multiple?
Private SaaS: 10-15x ARR at seed, 15-25x at Series A. Public SaaS: 5-15x revenue. Growth rate is the biggest factor in valuation.

What's the SaaS startup failure rate?
92% of SaaS startups fail within 3 years. Main causes: no market need (42%), cash problems (29%), wrong team (23%). Validated ideas have 3x better odds.

Quick Overview
SaaS businesses have unique characteristics that make validation both more important and more accessible than other business models. This guide walks you through a proven 5-step framework to validate your SaaS idea before writing a single line of code.

1
Why SaaS Validation is Different
SaaS businesses have unique characteristics that make validation both more important and more accessible than other business models.

Why it matters more:

High upfront development costs (3-12 months before launch)
Subscription model requires ongoing value delivery
Churn can kill even great products
Competition is intense in most niches
Why it's easier:

Digital products are easier to mock up
Landing page tests are highly effective
Target users are often online and reachable
Metrics like signups and trials provide clear signals
The goal of SaaS validation is to answer one question: Will enough people pay recurring fees for this solution?

2
Step 1: Validate the Problem Exists
Before building a solution, confirm the problem is real, painful, and frequent enough to warrant a SaaS product.

Problem validation checklist:

Can you describe the problem in one sentence?
Who experiences this problem? (Be specific)
How are they solving it today?
How often do they encounter it?
What's the cost of not solving it?
Red flags:

"Nice to have" vs "must have"
Problem occurs rarely
Existing solutions are good enough
Target users don't recognize the problem
How to validate:

Search Reddit, Twitter, and forums for complaints
Look for "workaround" solutions (spreadsheets, manual processes)
Check if competitors exist (competition = validated problem)
Talk to 10+ potential users
3
Step 2: Size Your Market (TAM/SAM/SOM)
A real problem with a tiny market won't build a viable business. Calculate your addressable market.

Market sizing framework:

TAM (Total Addressable Market): Everyone who could use your product
SAM (Serviceable Addressable Market): Those you can realistically reach
SOM (Serviceable Obtainable Market): What you can capture in 1-3 years
For SaaS, aim for:

SOM of at least $1M ARR potential
Market growing >10% annually
Not dominated by a single player
Quick market sizing methods:

Top-down: Industry reports ‚Üí segment percentage
Bottom-up: # of customers √ó average contract value
Value theory: Cost of problem √ó willingness to pay

4
Step 3: Analyze the Competition
Competition validates demand. No competition often means no market. Here's how to analyze effectively:

Competitor research framework:

Direct competitors: Same solution, same customer
Indirect competitors: Different solution, same problem
Alternative solutions: Spreadsheets, manual processes, agencies
What to look for:

Pricing models and price points
Feature gaps and weaknesses
Customer reviews (especially complaints)
Company size and funding
How long they've been in market
Finding your angle:

Underserved segment (niche down)
Better UX/simpler product
Lower price point
Specific integration or workflow
Geographic focus
Tools: G2, Capterra, SimilarWeb, BuiltWith, Crunchbase

5
Step 4: Test with a Landing Page
A landing page test gives you real data on whether people want your product before you build it.

Landing page validation formula:

Create a landing page describing your product
Drive traffic via ads ($100-500)
Measure conversion rates
Collect email signups or pre-orders
What to include:

Clear headline stating the value proposition
Problem statement that resonates
Key features/benefits
Social proof (if available)
Strong CTA (waitlist, pre-order, or demo request)
Success benchmarks:

Landing page ‚Üí signup: 10%+ is good
Signup ‚Üí paid intent: 20%+ is good
Cost per signup: <$10 for B2B SaaS
Pro tip: Create 2-3 variations and test different value propositions. The winning angle becomes your positioning.

6
Step 5: Customer Interviews
Quantitative data tells you what. Interviews tell you why. Both are essential.

Interview best practices:

Talk to 15-20 potential customers minimum
Ask about their current workflow and pain points
Don't pitch‚Äîlisten and probe
Focus on past behavior, not future intentions
Record and transcribe for analysis
Questions that reveal truth:

"Walk me through how you handle [problem] today."
"What's the most frustrating part of that process?"
"How much time/money does this cost you?"
"Have you tried to solve this before? What happened?"
"If this problem disappeared tomorrow, what would change?"
Red flags in interviews:

"That would be nice to have"
Can't describe current workflow
Problem is hypothetical, not lived experience
Unwilling to schedule follow-up
The Mom Test rule: People will lie to be nice. Ask about past behavior, not future intentions.
