# Marc Andreessen

## Core Philosophy

Marc Andreessen, cofounder of Netscape and partner at a16z, holds a central and forceful thesis: **the only thing that really matters in a startup is achieving Product-Market Fit (PMF)**. In his classic essays he states that almost all successes are explained by having found a real market with an intense need and a product capable of satisfying it. From this perspective, the **market rules**: a great market can compensate for an imperfect product or average team, while no brilliant team or excellent product can save a startup trapped in a small or nonexistent market.

His second great idea is that **software is eating the world**. Andreessen observes a structural transformation where software invades traditional industries, redefines business models, and creates entirely new categories. This drives his bias toward *builder* founders and ambitious visions: it's not about incremental improvements, but about building solutions that change the rules of the game in large and expanding markets. For Andreessen, thinking small is a bigger risk than thinking big.

Together, his philosophy combines **obsession with PMF**, **absolute primacy of the market**, and **technological optimism**. The founder's mission is to quickly find product-market fit in a large opportunity and then scale with aggressiveness before the window closes.

## Key Frameworks

### **Product-Market Fit**
**Andreessen's perspective:** PMF is being in a good market with a product that satisfies it. It's binary: you either have it or you don't.  
**How to evaluate:** Organic demand, accelerated growth, customers who return and recommend, operational pressure from excess demand.  
**PMF signals:** Sales that grow on their own, word of mouth, waiting lists, urgent need to hire.  
**No-PMF signals:** Sporadic use, high churn, forced sales, market indifference.

### **The Only Thing That Matters**
**Perspective:** PMF > product > team. Lack of market is the main startup killer.  
**How to evaluate:** Honest question: if I execute perfectly, can this market make me big?  
**Key signals:** If the market pulls the product, you're good; if you're pushing the market, not yet.

### **Market Size and Quality**
**Perspective:** The size and quality of the market determine the company's ceiling.  
**How to evaluate:** Large TAM, accessible SAM, relevant SOM in the short term; real pain and budget.  
**Positive signals:** Customers dissatisfied with current alternatives, urgency for solutions.  
**Negative signals:** Hobby markets, marginal budgets, low purchase frequency.

### **Software Eating the World**
**Perspective:** Software redefines entire industries.  
**How to evaluate:** Analog processes, inefficient intermediaries, bad UX, low automation.  
**Opportunity signals:** Slow incumbents, high friction, regulation leverageable via software.

### **Time to PMF**
**Perspective:** Speed matters; time without PMF is existential risk.  
**How to evaluate:** Months iterating vs. clear traction signals.  
**Warning signals:** Long cycles without real learning, high burn without adoption improvements.

### **Network Effects and Moats**
**Perspective:** After PMF, defensibility defines long-term value.  
**How to evaluate:** Does each new user make the product better?  
**Strong signals:** Network effects, accumulated data, high switching costs.  
**Weak signals:** Easy copying, low retention, exclusive dependence on paid marketing.

## Signature Tactics

1. **Detect PMF early**  
   **Framework:** Observe organic traction and pressure to scale.  
   **Questions:** Would users suffer if the product disappears? Does demand exceed supply?

2. **Choose market before product**  
   **Framework:** Market > product > team.  
   **Questions:** Does this problem really hurt? Are there enough buyers?

3. **Evaluate TAM/SAM/SOM rigorously**  
   **Framework:** Top-down + bottom-up.  
   **Questions:** Who are my first 100 customers? How much do they pay today?

4. **Pivot market vs. product**  
   **Framework:** Non-existent pain → market pivot; weak solution → product pivot.  
   **Questions:** Does the problem matter? Does my solution convince?

5. **Think competition from the market**  
   **Framework:** In large markets there are multiple winners.  
   **Questions:** Is there real demand? Is my focus on users or rivals?

6. **Scale aggressively post-PMF**  
   **Framework:** Capital, talent, and speed.  
   **Questions:** What stops me from growing 10x? Do I have enough runway?

7. **Ensure runway until PMF**  
   **Framework:** Spend to learn, not to look good.  
   **Questions:** Does my burn give me real time to iterate? Does each spend bring me closer to PMF?

8. **Think big**  
   **Framework:** Big problems attract better results.  
   **Questions:** Can this be huge? Does leading it for 10 years excite me?
