# Paul Graham

## Core Philosophy

Paul Graham (PG) argues that a startup is not defined by its size or age, but by its **growth**. A startup is a company designed to grow fast. Everything else is secondary. From this definition follows his most famous principle: *"Make something people want."* If you build something that a small group of users truly loves, growth appears as a natural consequence. It's not about marketing or early storytelling, but about solving a real problem significantly better than existing alternatives.

PG strongly emphasizes the role of **technical founders**. Not because non-technical people can't found startups, but because those who can build the product directly have a brutal advantage in iteration speed, problem understanding, and adaptability. Many of the best startups are born when founders solve a problem they themselves suffer, using skills others don't have. That initial asymmetry is key.

Much of his ideas are counterintuitive. He introduces concepts like **schlep blindness** to explain why many good ideas seem bad at the start: they involve heavy, uncomfortable, or unglamorous work. Precisely because of that, they tend to be underexploited. For PG, successful startups don't avoid hard work: they use it as a defensive barrier. His vision of the early stage is raw, artisanal, and deeply human: talk to users, do manual things, survive long enough to find the right path.

## Key Frameworks

### Startup = Growth
**Explanation**  
A startup is a company designed to grow fast. Growth is not just another KPI: it's the definition itself.

**Practical application**
- Measure weekly growth (users, revenue).
- Aim for 5–10% weekly growth in early stage.
- Use growth as a compass to decide priorities.

**Common errors**
- Confusing activity with progress.
- Optimizing vanity metrics.
- Thinking any new company is a startup.

---

### Do Things That Don't Scale
**Explanation**  
Startups take off because founders manually push them at the start.

**Practical application**
- Get users one by one.
- Manual onboarding.
- Ultra personalized support.
- Direct sales made by founders.

**YC examples**
- Stripe installing payments on customer laptops.
- Airbnb photographing apartments door to door.

**Common errors**
- Wanting to automate too soon.
- Believing "if it doesn't scale, it doesn't matter."
- Expecting growth to happen on its own.

---

### Schlep Blindness
**Explanation**  
We tend to ignore ideas that involve heavy or unpleasant work, even if they're valuable.

**Practical application**
- Review ideas discarded as "trash."
- Look for problems where others don't want to get involved.
- Ask yourself: "What problem do I wish someone else would solve?"

**YC examples**
- Stripe facing banks and regulations.
- Airbnb dealing with logistics and trust.

**Common errors**
- Looking for "nice" or comfortable ideas.
- Over-analyzing obstacles before starting.

---

### The Idea Maze
**Explanation**  
The first idea is almost never the correct one. The value is in navigating toward PMF through iterations.

**Practical application**
- Treat ideas as hypotheses.
- Launch fast and adjust.
- Listen to real signals, not abstract opinions.

**Common errors**
- Falling in love with the initial idea.
- Ignoring uncomfortable feedback.
- Not exploring enough of the problem space.

---

### Ramen Profitability
**Explanation**  
Moment when the startup generates enough for founders to survive.

**Practical application**
- Keep costs minimum.
- Prioritize early revenue.
- Extend runway without investment.

**Common errors**
- Confusing it with final success.
- Spending as if investment were infinite.

---

### Default Alive vs Default Dead
**Explanation**  
If you continue as you are, do you reach profitability before running out of money?

**Practical application**
- Project income and expenses honestly.
- Reduce burn or increase growth.
- Have a clear Plan B.

**Common errors**
- Not knowing what state you're in.
- Hiring too soon.
- Assuming you'll raise another round.

---

### Founder Mode (2024)
**Explanation**  
Scaling doesn't mean becoming a corporate manager. Founders must continue operating as founders.

**Practical application**
- Maintain direct contact with product and users.
- Skip hierarchies when necessary.
- Get involved in key decisions.

**Examples**
- Brian Chesky reversing corporate structures at Airbnb.
- Steve Jobs working with the 100 key employees.

**Common errors**
- Adopting traditional management too soon.
- Confusing founder mode with micromanagement.

## Signature Tactics

### 1. Generate startup ideas
**Steps**
1. Identify problems you yourself suffer.
2. Look for real frictions, not abstract ideas.
3. Validate that others also have the problem.

**YC examples**
- Dropbox (file synchronization).
- Airbnb (accommodation shortage).

---

### 2. Validate by talking to users
**Steps**
1. Launch something minimal.
2. Talk 1:1 with real users.
3. Iterate fast based on feedback.

**YC examples**
- Segment pivoting from analytics to data routing.
- Slack born from a failed game.

---

### 3. Do things that don't scale
**Steps**
1. Manual onboarding.
2. Direct founder support.
3. Learn from each interaction.

**YC examples**
- Wufoo sending handwritten letters.
- Stripe integrating manually.

---

### 4. Know if you're default alive
**Steps**
1. Project expenses vs income.
2. Define if you survive without investment.
3. Adjust aggressively if you're default dead.

**YC examples**
- Airbnb keeping minimal team post-seed.
- Segment cutting and refocusing.

---

### 5. Write for YC
**Steps**
1. Be clear and direct.
2. Demonstrate real user learning.
3. Tell what you've already done, not just the idea.

---

### 6. Prioritize as founder
**Steps**
1. Product and users first.
2. Eliminate distractions.
3. Use growth as filter.

---

### 7. Hire first employees
**Steps**
1. Wait until it hurts.
2. Look for "mini-cofounders."
3. Prioritize attitude and autonomy.

**YC examples**
- GitHub hiring from their community.

---

### 8. Launch fast and iterate
**Steps**
1. Launch something imperfect.
2. Learn from real use.
3. Iterate frequently.

---

### 9. Maintain extreme frugality
**Steps**
1. Minimum costs.
2. Basic founder salary.
3. Generate early revenue.

**Examples**
- Airbnb selling cereal to survive.

---

### 10. Choose cofounders correctly
**Steps**
1. Real complementarity.
2. History of working together.
3. Difficult conversations early.

**YC examples**
- Stripe (Collison brothers).
- Wufoo (solid previous team).
