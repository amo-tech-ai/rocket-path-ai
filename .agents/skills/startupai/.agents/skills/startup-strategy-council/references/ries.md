# Eric Ries

## Core Philosophy

Eric Ries argues that a **startup** is not a small version of a large company with a fixed plan, but a **human institution designed to create something new under extreme uncertainty**. Therefore, "progress" is not measured by features delivered or hours worked, but by **validated learning**: real evidence (from the market) that you're solving a problem in a way that can sustain a business.

Traditional business plans fail in startups because they depend on untested assumptions. Instead of planning "perfectly" and executing blindly, Ries proposes treating the business as an **experiment system**: formulate hypotheses, build the minimum to test them, measure real behavior, and learn quickly.

The operating engine is the **Build–Measure–Learn** cycle: build the minimum to learn, measure actionable data, learn, and make a decision (iterate, pivot, or persevere). The advantage isn't being "smarter," it's **learning faster than others**.

## Key Frameworks

### 1) Build–Measure–Learn loop
**What it is**  
A short cycle to turn hypotheses into evidence: **build** something minimum, **measure** real behavior and **learn** to decide the next step.

**How to implement it**
1. Define a hypothesis (e.g., "If X, then Y will do Z").
2. Design the simplest experiment that tests it (MVP).
3. Launch to a clear segment (early adopters).
4. Instrument events and funnels.
5. Decide: iterate (optimize), pivot (change strategy) or persevere.

**Associated metrics**
- Cycle time (idea → data).
- Conversion by stage (view→signup→activation→retention).
- Cohort retention.
- Incremental change per experiment (∆).

**Common errors**
- Building without hypothesis (feature factory).
- Measuring "everything" without deciding.
- Iterating slowly (long cycles).
- Confusing feedback "opinion" with evidence (behavior).

---

### 2) Minimum Viable Product (MVP) — real definition
**What it is (and what it's NOT)**
- **It is:** the **minimum version** that allows **learning** about a critical hypothesis with real customers.
- **It is NOT:** "incomplete product poorly done." MVP doesn't justify bad experience in the core value proposition.

**How to implement it**
1. Choose the riskiest **Leap of Faith** (value or growth).
2. Define the observable behavior that would validate it.
3. Choose format: landing + payment/checkout, concierge, wizard-of-oz, prototype, demo, etc.
4. Deliver the core value with sufficient quality (even if backend is manual).
5. Launch, measure, learn.

**Associated metrics**
- Activation (Aha moment reached).
- Early retention (D1/D7/D30 depending on product).
- Conversion to payment / payment intent (pre-order, deposit).
- Reuse rate (signal of real value).

**Common errors**
- MVP "feature-poor" but without value.
- Building too much (inflated MVP).
- Using MVP to "get out fast" without instrumentation.
- Validating interest with surveys instead of behavior.

---

### 3) Validated Learning
**What it is**  
Learning demonstrated with evidence: learning is "validated" when the market shows measurable signals of value or growth.

**How to implement it**
1. Translate assumptions to testable hypotheses.
2. Define metric and success threshold before the test.
3. Run experiment (MVP).
4. Extract actionable conclusion ("this works / doesn't work / we don't know").

**Associated metrics**
- Cohort retention.
- Conversion to key action (activation).
- Willingness to pay (pricing tests, pre-orders).
- Repeated engagement (frequency).

**Common errors**
- "We learned" = nice opinions.
- Experimenting without success criteria.
- Confirmation bias (reinterpreting results).
- Not documenting learning (repeating errors).

---

### 4) Innovation Accounting
**What it is**  
A system to measure real progress under uncertainty: replaces "plan vs. actual" with "baseline → improvement → decision."

**How to implement it**
1. **Baseline:** launch MVP and measure initial state.
2. **Tune the engine:** iterate with experiments to improve the north star metric.
3. **Pivot/persevere:** decide based on trend and threshold.

**Associated metrics**
- Cohorts (retention and conversion per cohort).
- LTV / CAC (if paid engine).
- Viral coefficient (if viral engine).
- Churn (if sticky engine).

**Common errors**
- Vanity metrics (accumulated totals).
- Not separating cohorts (mixing old with new).
- Not defining thresholds → never decide.
- Dashboard without decision narrative.

---

### 5) Pivot vs Persevere
**What it is**  
A periodic decision: change strategy without abandoning vision (pivot) or continue refining (persevere).

**How to implement it**
1. Schedule "reviews" regularly (every N experiments or weeks).
2. Review trend vs. objectives (not isolated numbers).
3. Identify which hypothesis failed.
4. Choose type of pivot (segment, channel, pricing, feature-zoom, etc.).
5. Define new hypotheses and restart cycle.

**Associated metrics**
- Progress of north star metric vs baseline.
- Early PMF signals (retention, repetition, expansion).
- Unit economics (if there's monetization).

**Common errors**
- Persevering out of ego ("one more iteration" forever).
- Pivoting out of anxiety (without learning).
- Changing too many variables at once.

---

### 6) Leap of Faith Assumptions
**What it is**  
Assumptions that must be true for the business to work. The two classic ones:
- **Value hypothesis:** Does this solve an important problem?
- **Growth hypothesis:** How does it grow sustainably?

**How to implement it**
1. List assumptions by: problem, customer, proposal, channel, pricing, costs.
2. Prioritize by: impact (if false it kills you) + uncertainty.
3. Design specific tests for top 1–2.
4. Don't advance "roadmap" before validating the critical.

**Associated metrics**
- Value: retention, repetition, time to value (TTV), qualitative NPS + real usage.
- Growth: viral coefficient, CAC, channel conversion.

**Common errors**
- Validating "easy" things first.
- Assuming the problem is real without observing it.
- Not separating segment: "everyone" is not a customer.

---

### 7) Engine of Growth (Viral / Sticky / Paid)
**What it is**  
Three growth engines. You must choose the dominant one to avoid scattering.

**How to implement each**
- **Sticky (retention):** improve onboarding, core loop, usage frequency.
  - Queen metric: **churn / retention**.
- **Viral:** design invitations and sharing within normal use.
  - Queen metric: **viral coefficient (k)** and invitation rate.
- **Paid:** buy growth with positive unit economics.
  - Queen metric: **LTV > CAC** (and payback).

**Common errors**
- Trying all 3 at the same time.
- Scaling paid without retention (burning cash).
- "Forced" virality that doesn't fit the product.

## Signature Tactics (10)

### 1) Design a real MVP (not "incomplete product")
**Step by step**
1. Write the hypothesis (value or growth).
2. Define the behavior that validates it.
3. Choose the cheapest format (landing, concierge, wizard-of-oz).
4. Deliver the core with sufficient quality.
5. Instrument events.
6. Launch to early adopters.
7. Decide according to threshold.

**Checklist**
- [ ] Hypothesis written in one sentence
- [ ] Success threshold defined
- [ ] Segment defined
- [ ] Instrumentation ready
- [ ] Decision plan (yes/no)

**Example**  
A B2B startup believes "finance teams will pay for automatic reconciliation." MVP: landing with pricing + recorded demo + "schedule" button + manual onboarding (concierge). Validation: % who schedule + % who pay for pilot.

---

### 2) Define "the metric that matters now"
**Step by step**
1. Define your stage: pre-PMF / PMF / post-PMF.
2. Choose bottleneck: activation, retention, monetization, acquisition.
3. Define 1 north star metric + 2 support metrics.
4. Review weekly and decide experiments.

**Template**
- Stage:
- Bottleneck:
- North star metric:
- Support metrics:
- This week's experiments:

**Example**  
If you have traffic but no one returns → north star metric = D7 retention. Not "total users."

---

### 3) Structure a validation experiment
**Step by step**
1. Hypothesis
2. Variable to change (only 1)
3. Segment
4. Duration / sample
5. Metric + threshold
6. Result → decision

**Template**
- Hypothesis:
- Change:
- Segment:
- Metric:
- Threshold:
- Decision if passes:
- Decision if fails:

**Example**  
A/B test of onboarding: "if we show use case specific by industry, activation increases 20%."

---

### 4) Avoid vanity metrics with cohorts
**Step by step**
1. Identify vanity metrics in the dashboard.
2. Define cohorts by week/month.
3. Measure retention and conversion per cohort.
4. Report trends, not totals.

**Checklist**
- [ ] Active cohorts
- [ ] Funnel by cohort
- [ ] Retention by cohort
- [ ] "Aha" event defined

**Example**  
"Downloads went up" doesn't matter if retention dropped. Cohorts reveal the truth.

---

### 5) Decide pivot vs persevere without drama
**Step by step**
1. Define evaluation window (e.g., 6 weeks).
2. Look at trend vs baseline.
3. Ask: which hypothesis failed?
4. If failed, define small and testable pivot.
5. Restart cycle with new hypotheses.

**Template**
- Baseline:
- Objective:
- Result:
- Hypothesis invalidated:
- Type of pivot:
- New experiment:

**Example**  
If pricing doesn't convert but usage does → monetization pivot (model), not product pivot.

---

### 6) Practical innovation accounting (learning board)
**Step by step**
1. Create "learning backlog" (list of hypotheses).
2. For each hypothesis: experiment, metric, threshold.
3. Execute 1–2 per week.
4. Record outcomes and decisions.

**Template**
| Hypothesis | Experiment | Metric | Threshold | Result | Decision |

**Example**  
"Customers pay $49/month" → test with real checkout and deposit → if <3% converts, readjust.

---

### 7) Identify leap of faith assumptions (quickly)
**Step by step**
1. Write your model on 1 page.
2. Underline what "must be true."
3. Ranking impact x uncertainty.
4. Test top 1.

**Checklist**
- [ ] Value (real problem)
- [ ] Customer (concrete segment)
- [ ] Channel (how they arrive)
- [ ] Pricing (they pay)
- [ ] Costs (unit economics)

**Example**  
If you don't know if they pay, don't build advanced automations.

---

### 8) Choose the dominant growth engine
**Step by step**
1. Decide: sticky/viral/paid.
2. Define 1 queen metric.
3. Design 3 specific experiments for that engine.
4. Don't mix strategies until dominating it.

**Example**  
Sticky engine: obsession with retention and core loop before buying ads.

---

### 9) Do "genchi gembutsu" (go and see)
**Step by step**
1. Observe 5 users using the product.
2. Note frictions and workarounds.
3. Translate to hypotheses and experiments.
4. Repeat every 2 weeks.

**Checklist**
- [ ] 5 observed sessions
- [ ] Top 3 frictions
- [ ] 3 derived experiments

**Example**  
You discover the "problem" isn't the feature, it's onboarding and language.

---

### 10) Short delivery cycles (deploy + learning)
**Step by step**
1. Implement small releases (feature flags).
2. CI/CD and rollback.
3. Instrumentation per feature.
4. Weekly impact review.

**Example**  
If your cycle is monthly, you learn 12 times/year. If weekly, 52.

## Best For (When to activate this advisor)
- Validating a new idea (value, pricing, channel).
- Designing product and GTM experiments.
- Deciding whether to pivot or persevere.
- Installing correct metrics (cohorts, funnels).
- When you're spending resources without learning.

---

## Red Flags (When NOT to use)
- Post-PMF scaling: you've validated; now execute and scale.
- If the problem is vision/macro strategy (not validation).
- If your team is already in analysis paralysis: Lean used badly can make it worse.

---

## Synergies (Combines well with)
- **Paul Graham:** Intuition + user closeness + Lean rigor.
- **April Dunford:** Lean to find "what works" → positioning to scale.
- **Peter Thiel:** Bold vision + validation of critical assumptions without self-deception.

---

## Anti-patterns (Conflicts with)
- "Just build it and they will come."
- Extensive market research without real behavior.
- Pivoting out of anxiety (without learning).
- Vanity metrics as compass.
- Long roadmaps without hypotheses and measurement instruments.

---

## Valid Criticisms of Lean (and how to avoid falling)
1. **Excuse for not deciding:** "let's keep experimenting" forever.
   - Antidote: Clear thresholds + decision dates (pivot/persevere).
2. **Optimize locally:** improving small metrics without vision.
   - Antidote: Maintain vision + choose north star metric aligned to business.
3. **MVP misunderstood:** launching junk.
   - Antidote: Minimum in scope, not minimum in value.
4. **Over-experimentation:** A/B tests without direction.
   - Antidote: Learning backlog prioritized by leap of faith assumptions.
