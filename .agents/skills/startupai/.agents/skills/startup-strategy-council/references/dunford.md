# April Dunford

## Core Philosophy

April Dunford is the advisor for **POSITIONING and CATEGORY**: she helps make a product "obvious" for the right buyer. Her thesis is simple and brutal: **if the market doesn't understand what you are, what you compete against, and why you're different, all other marketing falls short**. Positioning is not "nice copy"; it's the **strategic decision** of *in what context you want customers to evaluate you*. That context (the category) is the frame that defines what comparisons the buyer will make, what expectations they bring, and what "value" will seem relevant to them.

Dunford clearly separates three concepts that startups constantly mix:

* **Positioning:** The *strategic frame* that defines how you position yourself in the customer's mind (category, alternatives, differential value, for whom).
* **Messaging:** The *verbal translation* of that positioning into pages, ads, emails, pitch, etc. (words).
* **Branding:** The *identity and signals* (visual, tone, reputation) that reinforce trust and consistency (perception).

Most startups have weak positioning without knowing it because they "inherit" a default category ("we are X") that **doesn't maximize their strength**, or because they compete against the wrong alternatives (they think they compete against the software leader, but actually compete against Excel, manual processes, agencies, or the "no decision"). The typical result: the prospect doesn't get it, they're compared wrong, and sales become an uphill battle.

## Key Frameworks

### 1) 5 Components of Positioning

The 5 components are April's base model. They're not copy: they're decisions.

#### A) Competitive Alternatives

**What it is:** The real options the customer would use if you didn't exist.

**How to execute:**

1. Identify your current "best-fit customers" (those who buy fast, retain, recommend).
2. For each one, answer: *"If this product didn't exist, what would you do?"*
3. List **direct and indirect** alternatives:

   * "Obvious" competitors
   * "Non-obvious" competitors (another type of tool that solves the same job)
   * DIY (Excel, scripts, manual processes)
   * Human service (agency, consultant)
   * Status quo ("do nothing" / "keep things the same")
4. Validate in real deals (pipeline): what appears in conversations? what's in procurement? what beats you?

**Expected output:**

* A prioritized list (top 3–7) of real alternatives + notes on *why* the buyer considers them.

---

#### B) Unique Attributes

**What it is:** Capabilities, properties, or approaches that you have and alternatives don't.

**How to execute:**

1. List unique attributes without judging ("feature dump").
2. Include not just features:

   * Model (pricing, deployment, time-to-value)
   * Exclusive integrations/partners
   * Own data/benchmarks
   * Team experience (if it translates to tangible advantage)
   * Constraints converted into advantage (e.g., "only for X")

**Expected output:**

* List of 10–30 "raw" differential attributes, grouped by theme.

---

#### C) Value (and Proof)

**What it is:** The value those attributes produce in the customer **and the evidence** that backs it.

**How to execute:**

1. For each attribute, ask: **"And so what?"** (result).
2. Turn it into measurable or verifiable benefit:

   * Time (saves X hours)
   * Risk (reduces errors)
   * Money (reduces costs / increases revenue)
   * Speed (time-to-decision / time-to-value)
   * Quality (less churn, better NPS)
3. Add proof:

   * Use case
   * Before/after metric
   * Testimony
   * Specific demo (not generic)

**Expected output:**

* 3–5 "value pillars" (differentiated benefits) + proof bullets for each.

---

#### D) Target Customer Characteristics

**What it is:** The type of customer for whom your value is **more critical** and who pays more.

**How to execute:**

1. Describe your best customers' traits:

   * Industry / vertical
   * Size / complexity
   * Operational maturity
   * Problem urgency
   * Constraints (compliance, security, SLAs)
2. Identify "fast buy" signals:

   * Trigger events
   * Phrases they say
   * Explicit operational pain
3. Define "anti-target" (who should NOT buy).

**Expected output:**

* ICP v1: 5–10 criteria + triggers + anti-ICP.

---

#### E) Market Category

**What it is:** The category in which **you want to be compared** (the frame).

**How to execute:**

1. Generate 3–6 category options:

   * Existing broad category (risk: unfair comparisons)
   * Subcategory / niche within the existing (ideal)
   * Adjacent category (changes the frame)
   * New category (only if it doesn't fit any)
2. For each option, evaluate:

   * What expectations does the buyer bring?
   * Who would your comparables be?
   * Does your differential become obvious or diluted?
3. Choose the one that:

   * Makes your differential value *more relevant*
   * Reduces "bad" comparisons
   * Accelerates understanding and decision

**Expected output:**

* Chosen category + "sub-category" or nuance + rationale.

### 2) Positioning Canvas

The canvas is the complete exercise integrating the 5 components in a workshop.

**How to execute (practical workshop):**

1. **Pre-work (before the workshop)**

   * Bring 5–10 calls/deals (won and lost)
   * Compile objections and frequently asked questions
   * List top customers (the best ones)
2. **Session 1: Alternatives + Attributes**

   * Map of real alternatives
   * Unique attributes dump
3. **Session 2: Value + ICP**

   * Convert attributes → value
   * Define ICP and anti-ICP
4. **Session 3: Category**

   * Explore category and subcategory options
   * Choose winning frame
5. **Closing**

   * Positioning document (internal)
   * List of things to validate in market (experiments)

**Expected output:**

* An internal "Positioning Doc" with:

  * Real alternatives
  * Differentials + value + proof
  * ICP + anti-ICP
  * Category and framing

### 3) Category Creation vs Category Positioning

Strategic decision: **do I create a category or position within an existing one?**

#### Category Positioning (enter an existing category)

**When to use:**

* You want speed of understanding
* There's limited budget to educate the market
* You can win in a niche within the category

**How to execute:**

* "I'm X, but for Y with Z differential value"
* Pattern example: *"CRM for enterprise sales teams in regulated industries"*

**Expected output:**

* Understandable frame + defensible niche.

#### Category Creation (create new category)

**When to use (rare):**

* Your product doesn't fit any category without distortion
* You're willing to pay the education cost
* You have strong evidence of a new way to solve the problem

**Risks:**

* High evangelization cost
* Others can capitalize on your education and beat you

**Expected output:**

* Category name + new problem + "why now" + education playbook.

### 4) Sales Pitch Structure (Sales Pitch, 2023)

April complements positioning with a sales pitch that **creates context before presenting product**.

#### Structure

1. **Setup**

   * **Insights:** Problem reframing / uncomfortable market truth
   * **Alternatives:** Map of options and trade-offs
   * **The Prize:** How the "perfect world" looks (desired outcome)
2. **Proof**

   * Evidence and demonstration (cases, metrics, focused demo)
3. **The Ask**

   * Clear next step (pilot, trial, next meeting, purchase)

> Note: In this structure, the product appears as the "embodiment" of the Prize, and Proof validates it's not hot air.

**How to execute (practical):**

* Design a deck with 5–8 slides maximum:

  * 1–2 slides of Insight
  * 1 slide of Alternatives (with trade-offs)
  * 1 slide of Prize (vision)
  * 2 slides of Proof (case + focused demo)
  * 1 slide of Ask (next step)
* Train the team to make it conversational (questions during Setup).

**Expected output:**

* Repeatable pitch that reduces "no decision" and accelerates clarity.

## Signature Tactics (10)

### 1) Identify *real* competitive alternatives (not the obvious ones)

**Step by step**

1. Review 10 recent deals (won/lost).
2. Extract what alternative was mentioned explicitly.
3. Do 5 quick customer interviews (15 min): "what did you use before?" "what did you consider?"
4. Classify alternatives by frequency and by "threat" (they beat you / they replace you).
5. Eliminate "ghost" competitors (never actually appear).

**Questions**

* What would you do if I disappeared tomorrow?
* What tool/process lets you *keep operating* even if badly?
* What stops you more: choosing another vendor or not changing?

**Template**

* Alternative | Why it's used | Perceived advantage | Limitation | How we win/lose

---

### 2) Find differentiated value (attributes → results)

**Step by step**

1. Take 10 unique attributes.
2. For each one, apply the chain:

   * Attribute → Capability → Outcome → Business impact
3. Write 1 proof per outcome (case, metric, testimony).
4. Reduce to 3–5 "value pillars."

**Questions**

* What does it enable that was impossible before?
* What speeds up, reduces, or avoids?
* Who in the client company celebrates that result?

**Template**

* Attribute: ___
* Outcome: "The client can ___"
* Impact: "That means ___"
* Proof: ___

---

### 3) Choose the correct market category (frame that favors you)

**Step by step**

1. Generate 5 possible category labels.
2. For each: list *comparables*, expectations, and typical objections.
3. Evaluate if your differential becomes:

   * Obvious (✅) / Irrelevant (❌)
4. Choose the category where you're the "natural leader" for the ICP.
5. Decide subcategory (vertical, use case, regulatory environment, etc.).

**Questions**

* In what category do I lose by default even though I'm good?
* In which one does my "advantage" become purchase criterion #1?
* What category makes them compare me against the right rivals?

**Template**

* Category | Comparables | Expectations | Our advantage | Risk

---

### 4) Run a positioning workshop (without chaos, with output)

**Step by step**

1. Convene: CEO + Product + Sales + Marketing (max 8).
2. Pre-work: 5 deals, top objections, 5 top clients.
3. Agenda: 2 sessions of 2h (better than 1 marathon).
4. Facilitate with rules:

   * "No features without value"
   * "No assumptions without evidence"
   * "We decide by ICP, not by ego"
5. Close with Positioning Doc v1 + validation backlog.

**Questions**

* Do we all agree on what we compete against?
* What is the #1 value the ICP buys?
* What phrase kills our clarity today?

**Template**

* Doc v1: Alternatives / Differentials / Value+Proof / ICP / Category / Anti-ICP

---

### 5) Translate positioning → messaging (without losing rigor)

**Step by step**

1. Convert the Positioning Doc into a "Messaging House":

   * 1 central idea (category + value)
   * 3 value pillars
   * 1 proof per pillar
2. Write:

   * Homepage one-liner
   * Subheadline (for ICP)
   * 3 value bullets
   * 3 proof bullets
3. Create versions by persona (CFO, Ops, IT, etc.).
4. Reinforce consistency (web, ads, emails, deck).

**Questions**

* Does the message answer "what is it" in 5 seconds?
* Is the ICP reflected or does it sound generic?
* Is the proof credible or does it look like empty promise?

**Template**

* One-liner: "We are [category] for [ICP] that delivers [value #1] thanks to [differential]."

---

### 6) Structure a sales pitch (Sales Pitch 2023) that wins

**Step by step**

1. Build Setup (Insight → Alternatives → Prize).
2. Prepare Proof: 1 case + 1 focused demo per pillar.
3. Define Ask (only 1 next step).
4. Practice 10 times with a stopwatch (15–20 min total).
5. Iterate according to real prospect questions.

**Questions**

* Am I helping the buyer decide or just describing my product?
* Is it clear why *change* now?
* Is the Ask aligned with confidence level?

**Template (short script)**

* Insight: "What usually happens is ___"
* Alternatives: "People solve it with A/B/C, but ___"
* Prize: "Ideally it would be ___"
* Proof: "Look at this case/data"
* Ask: "The next step is ___"

---

### 7) Test if positioning works (hard and soft signals)

**Step by step**

1. Internal test: can sales repeat it without reading?
2. Test with 5 prospects: "what do you think we do?" (after 30s).
3. Observe:

   * Do "what is this?" questions go down?
   * Does lead quality go up?
4. Measure:

   * demo-to-opportunity
   * win rate in ICP
   * sales cycle
5. Adjust: category, proof, or ICP focus.

**Questions**

* Are we being compared to who we want?
* Do we filter "bad fits" better?
* Did the objection change from "I don't understand" to "price/ROI"?

**Template**

* Signal | Before | After | Hypothesis | Adjustment

---

### 8) Find "edge cases" where your advantage is criterion #1

**Step by step**

1. List scenarios where your value is critical (e.g., compliance, latency, auditing).
2. Identify who lives that pain (role + industry).
3. Create micro-messages per scenario (landing + pitch).
4. Run small campaigns or targeted outreach.
5. Double down where response is immediate.

**Questions**

* Where does my feature stop being "nice-to-have" and become "must"?
* What segment has real urgency right now?

**Template**

* Scenario | Pain | Stakeholder | Message | Proof

---

### 9) Design winnable subcategory (without inventing complete category)

**Step by step**

1. If the category is large and crushes you, create subcategory:

   * Vertical (health, finance)
   * Use case (onboarding, compliance)
   * Environment (enterprise, regulated)
2. Name the subcategory clearly.
3. Align website + pitch + outbound to that subcategory.
4. Win "mindshare" there before expanding.

**Questions**

* Can I be #1 in something specific in 6–12 months?
* What niche values exactly my differential?

**Template**

* "We are [category] for [sub-niche] with [differential]."

---

### 10) Avoid repositioning as makeup (change reality or change nothing)

**Step by step**

1. If the problem is product, fix it before "renaming" it.
2. If you change category, update:

   * Pricing/packaging
   * Onboarding
   * Relevant success stories
3. Roll out in phases (pilot → web → ads → sales).
4. Maintain internal and external coherence.

**Questions**

* Does the product really deliver the promise of the new frame?
* Do we have proof in the new ICP?
* What part of the journey contradicts the new positioning?

**Template**

* New frame | Required changes | Risks | Rollout plan | Success metric

## Best For (When to activate this advisor)

* Defining or redefining positioning (focus change, new ICP, new offer).
* Preparing launch (product, major feature, rebrand with strategic change).
* When the message isn't resonating (confusion, low conversion, wrong comparisons).
* Training sales team (deck, demo, talk track, objections).
* When you want to design a winnable subcategory or evaluate category creation.

---

## Red Flags (When NOT to use)

* **Pre-PMF:** Before you need problem/value validation; positioning without evidence is hot air.
* When the problem is the product (bad retention, value not delivered, broken UX).
* "There's no competition": there usually is (status quo). If literally none, you're in market education mode (another strategy, much more expensive).

---

## Synergies (Combines well with)

* **Thiel:** "Monopoly" = perfect positioning in a niche where you're #1 (winnable category).
* **Marc Andreessen:** PMF + positioning = efficient scaling (clarity + distribution).
* **Eugene Schwartz (awareness levels):** Positioning defines the frame; Schwartz defines **how** to communicate it according to awareness (problem/solution/product).

---

## Anti-patterns (Conflicts with)

* **"We're like X but better":** It condemns you to playing the leader's game, with its rules.
* **Positioning by features:** Describes *things*, not *reasons to buy*.
* **Changing positioning without changing reality:** Creates dissonance, churn, and loss of credibility.
* Bonus: **Too broad category** ("we're AI for companies") = guaranteed invisibility.
